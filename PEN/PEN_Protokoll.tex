\section{Physical Background for Task 2}
From Task 1 we know the solution to our equation of motion and therefore the value of $T_0$:
\begin{equation}
T_{0} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I}{Mgs}}
\end{equation}
Also, we remember the Steiner theorem:
\begin{equation}
I_{new} = I_{known} + Ms^2
\end{equation}
where $s$ is the displacement of the knew axis relative to the old.

We consider the center of mass and two displacements $l_a$ and $l_b$ of the axis. We know $l_a + l_b = d$, the distance between our two axis.

With (1) and (2) we get (3), which holds for $l_a$ and $l_b$:
\begin{equation}
T_a = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I_a}{Mgl_a}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I_{cm} + Ml_a^2}{Mgl_a}}
\end{equation}

By moving our additional mass on the pendulum we can move the center of mass. Assuming we can find a position (and it turns out that this position can easily be found) where our measured periods are the same, we find: $T_a = T_b$!
Now the periods can easily be plugged in and the resulting equation yields:
\begin{equation}
I_{cm} = Ml_al_b
\end{equation} 
Pluggin that into our $T_0$ we find:
\begin{align}\
T & = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I_{cm} + Ml_a^2}{Mgl_a}}\nonumber \\
T & = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{Ml_al_b + Ml_a^2}{Mgl_a}}\nonumber \\ 
T & = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l_a + l_b}{g}}\nonumber \\ 
T & = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{d}{g}}\nonumber
\end{align}
\begin{equation}
\Longrightarrow g = d(\frac{2\pi}{T})^2
\end{equation}

Since $d$ and $T$ are easily measured, this is what we need!

\section{Auswertung}
\subsection{Task 1}
In Task 1 the period $T$ is measured in comparison to the amplitude of the pendulum. In \emph{Graph 1} we evaluate our measurement results.

The input errors for $\frac{d^2}{s^2}$ and $T$ are:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\frac{d^2}{s^2} = \sqrt{(\frac{2d}{s^2}\Delta d)^2 + (\frac{-2d^2}{s^3}\Delta s)^2}=
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\Delta T = 0.001s
\end{equation}
However the error in $d^2/s^2$ is almost too small to be plottet. From the Graph, the value of $T_0$ is found and an error is obtained:
\begin{equation}
T_0 = (1.972 \pm 0.002) s
\end{equation}

\subsection{Task 2}
In Task 2 the gravitational constant $g$ is determined with equation (5). Please refer to the physical background section for a thorough motivation of this:
\begin{equation}
g = d(\frac{2\pi}{T})^2
\end{equation}
\[ g = 0.9941m(\frac{2\pi}{2.0049s})^2=9.7647\frac{m}{s^2}\]
The error of $g$ is:
\begin{equation}
\Delta g = 2\pi\sqrt{(\frac{1}{T^2}\Delta d)^2 + (\frac{-2d}{T^3}\Delta T)^2}
\end{equation}
\[ \Delta g = 0.0005\frac{m}{s^2}\]
The result is:
\[ g = (9.7647 \pm 0.0005) \frac{m}{s^2}\]

\section{Conclusion}
In task 1 we measured the period to be $T_0 = 1.972(2)s$. For comparison with the theoretical value of a linear body we use:
\[T_0 = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I + ms^2}{msg}} $with$ I=\frac{1}{12}mL^2\]
and find:
\[T_0 = 1.970s\]
This approximation seems to be legit and is congruent with our result.

In task 2 we determine the gravitational constant $g = 9.7647(5)ms^{-2}$. The literature value is $9.8128ms^{-2}$. Although our value of $g$ seems reasonably good, our error has been calculated to be extremely small and so the triple error intervall still doesn't overlap with the literature value of $g$.
However if friction has to be taken into account, it should lead us to measure a $g$ that is smaller than the true $g$ and that is the case here, so we guess that friction cannot be entirely ignored.
